## The Yassir Comparison

Yassir started in 2019 with a small team in Algiers and grew to operate across Africa. They did not start by trying to be everywhere — they started in one neighbourhood, proved it worked, then expanded. ILMI needs to follow the same logic:

**Own one city → prove it works → expand.**

## 1. Understanding Your Market

### 1.1 The Problem You Are Solving

Algerian schools are managed through a combination of paper registers, WhatsApp groups, phone calls, and manual notebooks. Parents find out their child is absent days later. Teachers have no unified system for recording grades. School administrators cannot see attendance data without walking into classrooms.

ILMI replaces all of this with one platform. This is not a nice-to-have product — it solves a daily frustration experienced by millions of people across Algeria every single day.

---

### 1.2 Who Are Your Customers?

You have two types of customers and you must think about both separately.

**The Buyer — School Administrators**
This is the person who pays you. They are the principal, the director, or the owner of a private school. They feel the pain of managing a school manually every day. They want to appear modern and professional to parents. They are your primary sales target.

**The User — Parents, Teachers, Students**
These people do not pay you, but they determine whether the product succeeds. If parents love the app, they will pressure school directors to keep using it. If teachers find it easy, they will promote it. Users are your best salespeople once the product is in a school.

---

### 1.3 Types of Schools to Target

| School Type | Priority | Why | Typical Size |
|-------------|----------|-----|-------------|
| Private primary schools | 🔥 Highest | Owners make decisions quickly, parents are engaged, fees justify paying for software | 200–600 students |
| Private middle & high schools | 🔥 High | Same as above, slightly larger, more complex needs | 300–900 students |
| Private language institutes | Medium | Smaller, simpler, good for early testing | 50–200 students |
| Public schools | Low (for now) | Slow procurement, bureaucratic, no budget authority at school level | 500–2000 students |

> ⚠️ **Start exclusively with private schools.** Public schools involve the Ministry of Education, procurement committees, and processes that can take years. Private school owners can say yes in one meeting.

---

### 1.4 Algeria Education Numbers

Algeria has over 27,000 schools. Even a very conservative estimate puts the number of private schools that could benefit from ILMI at several thousand. You only need to sign **10 schools to prove the concept**. You only need **50 schools to have a real business**. The market is enormous relative to what you need to start.

## 2. Your Business Model

### 2.1 How You Make Money — SaaS Subscription

SaaS stands for Software as a Service. Instead of selling your software once, you charge schools a recurring monthly or annual fee to use it. This is the best business model for your product because:

- Predictable revenue every month — you know what you will earn
- Schools become long-term customers, not one-time buyers
- The software improves over time and justifies the continued payment
- Switching costs are high — once a school's data is in your system, they are unlikely to leave

---

### 2.2 Pricing Structure

Price based on the number of students in the school. This feels fair to the buyer and scales your revenue automatically as they grow.

| Plan | School Size | Monthly Price | Annual Price | What's Included |
|------|-------------|--------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Starter** | Up to 300 students | 3,000 DZD | 30,000 DZD | All roles, basic features, email support |
| **Pro** | Up to 700 students | 6,000 DZD | 60,000 DZD | Everything in Starter + AI features, priority support |
| **Pro AI** | 700+ students | 10,000 DZD | 100,000 DZD | Full platform, unlimited users, dedicated onboarding |

> ⚠️ **Start With Lower Prices.** Your first 10 schools are pilot customers, not full paying customers. Offer 50–70% discounts or 3 months free in exchange for honest feedback and a testimonial. You are buying proof, not revenue at this stage.

---


### 2.4 The Free Tier Question

Should you have a free plan? The answer is **no** in the traditional sense, but **yes** in a different sense. Instead of a permanent free plan that trains customers to never pay you, offer:

- A **30-day free trial** for every new school — no credit card required
- A **free pilot program** for the first 5 schools in exchange for structured feedback and a case study
- A **referral discount** — schools that refer other schools get one month free

## 3. Go-To-Market Strategy

Go-to-market means: how do you get your first customers? This is where most technical founders fail — they build a great product and then wait for customers to appear. They do not appear. You have to go find them.

---

### 3.1 Phase 0 — Before You Launch (Months 1–2)

Before you try to sell anything, you need social proof and credibility. No school director will give money to three unknown students with a website. You need to become known first.

#### Step 1 — Build Your Online Presence (All Free)

- LinkedIn profiles for all three founders — professional photos, clear bio mentioning ILMI
- A LinkedIn company page for ILMI
- An Instagram account (@ilmidz) — for behind-the-scenes content
- A Facebook page — critical for Algeria, many school directors are on Facebook
- A simple one-page landing page website with a contact form

#### Step 2 — Document Everything You Build

Start posting content about what you are building from day one. This is called **building in public**. Post things like:

- *"We just finished the grade management feature — here is how it works"*
- *"Problem we solved this week: a teacher can now take attendance in 30 seconds instead of 5 minutes"*
- *"We interviewed 3 school directors this week. Here is what they told us about managing a school in Algeria"*

This content attracts your future customers before your product is ready. It positions you as experts. It costs nothing.

#### Step 3 — Interview 20 School Directors Before Selling

Contact 20 private school directors and ask for 20 minutes of their time. Tell them you are students building software for Algerian schools and you want to understand their problems — **you are NOT selling anything yet**. Ask them:

- What is the most painful part of managing your school day to day?
- How do you currently communicate with parents about grades and attendance?
- Have you tried any software before? What happened?
- What would make you willing to pay for a software solution?

> 💡 **The Secret Benefit of Interviews:** When you go back to these 20 school directors with a demo, 30–40% of them will convert into paying customers. They feel invested in your product because they helped shape it. You built a relationship before the sales conversation.

---

### 3.2 Phase 1 — Your First 5 Schools (Months 2–4)

Your only goal in this phase is to get 5 schools **using** the product. Not paying — using. Data inside the system, teachers logging in, parents getting notifications.

#### How to Get the First 5

1. Your personal network first — does anyone know a school director? A teacher? A parent who knows a director? One warm introduction is worth 100 cold messages.
2. The schools your family members' children attend — you already have a relationship there
3. Private schools in your wilaya — visit in person, ask to speak to the director, bring a laptop to demo
4. WhatsApp groups for educators — Algeria has many teacher and school administrator groups, join them and share your product
5. LinkedIn outreach — message school directors directly with a short, personal message

#### What to Offer the First 5 — The Pilot Offer Script

> *"We are ILMI, a team of Algerian students building school management software. We want to give your school 3 months completely free access in exchange for your honest feedback and permission to use your school as a case study. We will set everything up for you, train your staff, and be available whenever you need help. There is no risk and no commitment."*

Almost every school director will say yes to this. It costs them nothing. You get real users, real feedback, and a real success story to show the next school.

---

### 3.3 Phase 2 — First Paying Customers (Months 4–8)

Once you have 5 schools using the product and happy with it, you have everything you need to sell. You have testimonials, data, and proof that it works.

#### The Sales Playbook

1. Identify target schools — private schools in your wilaya with 200–600 students
2. Find the decision maker — the director, principal, or owner
3. Send a warm message — reference the nearby school that is already using ILMI
4. Request a 30-minute demo meeting — in person if possible, video call if not
5. Run the demo — show the most impressive features first: live parent notifications, grade management, attendance in seconds
6. Address objections — see the table below
7. Close with a clear offer — *"Would you like to start a free 30-day trial?"*
8. Onboard them immediately — do not wait, set everything up while enthusiasm is high

#### Common Objections and How to Answer Them

| Objection | Answer |
|-----------|--------|
| *"We already use WhatsApp for communication"* | WhatsApp is great for messaging but has no data, no records, no structure. When a parent asks what grade their child got in October, where do you find that? With ILMI, it's one click. |
| *"The teachers won't use it"* | We train all your teachers for free. We make it so simple that a teacher who has never used a smartphone app can learn it in one session. |
| *"It's too expensive"* | At 3,000 DZD per month for 300 students, that is 10 DZD per student per month — less than the cost of a piece of paper. And we offer a 30-day free trial. |
| *"We tried software before and it didn't work"* | Show them specifically how ILMI is different. Offer the free trial so they have nothing to lose. |
| *"I need to think about it"* | Ask: "What specific question would help you decide?" Then answer it. Offer to set up a small demo with just the director and one teacher to see it in action. |

---

### 3.4 Phase 3 — Scaling to 50+ Schools (Months 8–18)

By month 8, if you have 10–15 paying schools, you have proven the model. Now you scale.

#### Word of Mouth System

The most powerful growth channel in Algeria is word of mouth. School directors talk to each other. Build this into your product:

- When a school is happy, explicitly ask: *"Do you know any other school directors we should speak to?"*
- Create a referral program: a school that refers another school gets one free month
- Feature happy schools in your social media content (with their permission)

#### Expand Geographically

Start in your own wilaya. Once you have 10 schools there, move to adjacent wilayas. **Do not try to cover all of Algeria at once** — you cannot service schools you cannot visit. Grow like a stain on a map: dense in the center, expanding outward.

#### Hire a Sales Representative

Once you have revenue, hire one part-time sales representative in each new wilaya you enter. A local person with local relationships can sign 5–10 schools per month. Pay them a base salary plus commission per school signed.

## 5. Product Strategy

### 5.1 What to Build First vs. What to Build Later

The temptation will be to keep adding features. Resist it. More features before you have customers is a trap — you are building things nobody has asked for.

#### Must Have Before First Sale ✅

- Stable, working login for all roles
- Grade entry and parent visibility
- Attendance tracking
- Basic push notifications to parents
- Simple, clean onboarding for a new school

#### Build After First 10 Schools Give Feedback 🔜

- Report generation (end-of-term reports, attendance reports)
- In-app messaging between teachers and parents
- Homework assignment and submission
- Timetable / schedule management
- Fee tracking and payment reminders

#### Build at Scale (After 50+ Schools) 🚀

- AI features (automated insights, predictive attendance)
- Ministry of Education reporting integration
- Multi-language support (Arabic, French, Tamazight)
- Advanced analytics dashboard for school directors

---

### 5.2 The Onboarding Problem

The biggest reason schools stop using software is because onboarding is hard. If getting started feels complicated, they give up. Your onboarding must be so simple that a director can set up their school in **30 minutes without calling you**.

Invest heavily in:
- A step-by-step setup wizard in the admin panel
- Short video tutorials in Arabic for each feature
- A WhatsApp number they can message for help
- In-person onboarding for the first 20 schools — go to the school, set everything up yourself

---

### 5.3 Customer Success — The Key to Retention

In SaaS, the real money is in **keeping** customers, not acquiring new ones. A school that stays for 3 years pays you 3x what a school that leaves after one year pays.

Build these habits into your team from day one:
- Monthly check-in call or message with every paying school
- Track which schools are not using the app actively — reach out before they cancel
- Send a monthly email showing the school their usage statistics (*"Your teachers recorded 1,247 grades this month"*)
- Ask for feedback every quarter and show customers that you acted on their feedback

## 6. Legal & Official Setup

You do not need to be fully legally incorporated before getting your first customers. But you should start the process early because some school directors will ask for it before signing a contract.

---

### 6.1 Register as a Company in Algeria — SARL

The most practical legal structure for you is a **SARL (Société à Responsabilité Limitée)** — the Algerian equivalent of a limited liability company. It protects the founders personally — if the company owes money, creditors cannot take your personal assets.

**Steps to register:**

1. Choose a company name and verify it is not taken (check with CNRC — Centre National du Registre de Commerce)
2. Draft the statutes (founding documents) — a notary can help with this
3. Open a bank account in the company name and deposit the minimum capital
4. Register with CNRC to get your Registre de Commerce number
5. Register with the tax authority (DGI) to get your NIF (Numéro d'Identification Fiscale)
6. Register with social security (CNAS/CASNOS) when you start paying yourselves

> 💡 **Algeria Startup Label:** Algeria has a Startup Label program managed by the Ministry of Knowledge Economy. Getting this label gives you **tax exemptions for up to 4 years**, access to funding, and significant credibility. Apply at startup.dz after you register your company.

---

### 6.2 Contracts With Schools

Every school that uses ILMI should sign a contract. The contract should cover:

- What the software does (scope of service)
- How much they pay and when
- Data privacy — who owns the school's data (the school owns their data, always)
- What happens if you need to take the service down for maintenance
- How either party can end the contract

You do not need a lawyer for your first contracts — find a template online and adapt it. Get a lawyer once you have revenue.

---

### 6.3 Data Privacy

ILMI stores sensitive data about children. This is a serious responsibility. Adopt these practices from day one:

- Never sell or share school data with any third party — ever
- Only collect data that the product actually needs
- Allow schools to export and delete all their data at any time
- Write a clear, simple privacy policy in Arabic and French

## 7. Team Structure & Dividing Responsibilities

Three founders is a strong starting team. But if all three of you do the same things, nothing gets done at scale. **Divide your roles clearly from day one.**

| Role | Primary Focus | Secondary Focus |
|------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Founder 1 — CEO / Sales** | Customer meetings, demos, closing deals, managing school relationships | Content creation, partnerships |
| **Founder 2 — CTO / Backend** | Django backend, API, server, database, security | Code reviews, technical decisions |
| **Founder 3 — Product / Mobile** | Flutter app, React frontend, UI/UX, onboarding | Customer feedback, feature prioritization |

Every week, the team should have one structured meeting covering three questions:
- What did we accomplish this week?
- What are we doing next week?
- What is blocking us?

### 9.3 When and How to Raise Investment

After 6–12 months with 10–20 paying schools and consistent monthly revenue, you are in a strong position. In Algeria, your options are:

**Algeria Startup Fund**
The Algerian government has a dedicated fund for startups. With the Startup Label, you can access grants and loans at favorable rates. This is your first funding target because it does not require giving up equity.

**Angel Investors**
Successful Algerian business owners who invest in early-stage companies. Find them through startup events, LinkedIn, and the Algeria startup ecosystem. Come with data: number of schools, retention rate, monthly revenue, growth rate.

**Accelerator Programs**
Programs like Orange Fab and Djezzy Foundation provide funding plus mentorship. They take a small equity stake (typically 5–10%) in exchange for support and network access. Excellent for first-time founders.

## 10. Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why It Kills Startups | What to Do Instead |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **Building features nobody asked for** | You waste months building something that does not solve a real problem | Talk to 20 school directors before building anything new. Build only what multiple customers have specifically asked for. |
| **Waiting until the product is perfect to launch** | Perfect never comes. You miss 6 months of real feedback. | Launch when 3 schools can genuinely use the core features. Imperfect and real beats perfect and theoretical. |
| **Targeting everyone at once** | You spread yourself too thin and own nothing | Own one wilaya first. Own private primary schools first. Then expand. |
| **Not following up after a demo** | 80% of sales happen after the 3rd follow-up. Most founders give up after one. | Schedule the next contact before leaving every meeting. Follow up 3 times minimum. |
| **Underpricing out of fear** | Low prices attract price-sensitive customers who leave at the first better offer | Price based on the value you deliver. 3,000 DZD for 300 students is already affordable. |
| **Not documenting customer feedback** | You repeat the same conversations and forget what matters most | After every customer meeting, write 5 bullet points of what they said, what they need, what surprised you. |
| **Founding team conflict** | More startups fail from cofounder disagreements than from bad products | Agree in writing on equity split, roles, and what happens if someone wants to leave. Do this now, before there is money. |
